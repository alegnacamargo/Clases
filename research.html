I'm involving in several projects related with the estimation of phylogenetic relationships and species delimitation in Anolis lizards. As a result of these projects, we have discovered new species (e.g.,  Anolis anoriensis , Anolis limon and Anolis otongae) and validated older William's nomen nudum species (e.g., Anolis anchicayae, Anolis lyra and Anolis urraoi). Some of these new species have been discovered after extensive fieldwork in unexplored areas in Colombia and also after the detailed examination of specimens deposited in herpetological collections. Both fieldwork and checking specimens in jars provide excellent opportunities to discover the hidden anole fauna in Colombia (a both museum and cradle of anole diversity). I have collaborated in the generation of a large-scale anole phylogeny combining morphological and DNA data (Poe et al. 2017).

Establish the causes of geographical patterns of species richness gradients and endemism is an interesting topic in ecology and biogeography.  Using macroecological and macroevolutionary approaches I aim to evaluate the roles of climate and evolutionary process driving species richness patterns in Anolis lizards. I've conducted spatially explicit simulations to generate species richness gradients with the aim to evaluate whether resulting richness-climate relationships are a causal or spurious basis. ​Also, I'm interested in predict how anole species assemblages have changed through time using a SESAM framework (Guisan and Rahbek 2011). I'm generating ecological niche models for almost 300 anole species using a comprehensive database with ~14000 georeferenced occurrence records compiled from several sources. 
​
Another topic of my interest deals with evaluate the role of historical and ecological factors driving exceptional endemism in Caribbean amphibians and reptiles.. In particular, it is well-known that almost all Caribbean Anolis lizards are endemic to a single island.. Using a calibrated phylogeny and new parametric biogeographic methods, I've reconstructed a historical biogeographic scenario for Caribbean anole lizards. Using ecological niche models -ENMs- I estimated whether single-island endemics exhibit potential ecological conditions outside its native areas. ENMs and historical biogeography facilitates disentangle the relative role of historical and climatic factors underlying exceptional endemism in Caribbean groups.

The relationship between climatic niche attributes and species diversification is one of the research topics more interesting in evolutionary biology, Using a model-based phylogenetic comparative approach, we have tested for a link between climatic niche attributes and species diversification in Anolis lizards. These results suggest that climate play a role in anole diversification dependent of geographical context. I will explore whether other taxonomic groups (e.g., frogs, birds) exhibit the same macroevolutionary patterns. Also, we are exploring whether different traits exhibit similar or different evolutionary trajectories and how these can have an impact on diversification dynamics.


Climatic niche and diversification

The evaluation of the effect of the current and past climate on species distribution is a key step to designe appropriate conservation strategies. During my postdoctoral time I will evaluated how past climate have affected geographical patterns of species richness and endemism in several groups of amphibian and reptiles in Middle America.. Also, I will evaluate whether geographical biodiversity patterns for these groups can be explained by only range cohesiveness, niche conservatism or a combination of both. I'm using a combination of cutting-edge modeling tools (macroecology, ecological niche modeling, GIS).
